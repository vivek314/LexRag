The problem

The same question, two answers — one of them illegal-to-rely-on.

India replaced the Income-tax Act, 1961 with the Income-tax Act, 2025 (in force 1 Apr 2026). Section numbers everyone memorised changed. But the internet — and most documents an assistant ingests — still describe the old law.

❌ Stale source (2020 booklet)

“Claim your life-insurance & PPF deduction under Section 80C of the Income Tax Act, 1961.”

Confident. Well-written. Wrong as current law.

✓ Authoritative source (2025 Act)

The deduction is under Section 123, read with Schedule XV, capped at ₹1,50,000.

“80C” returns zero operative hits in the 2025 Act.

Why naive RAG fails here

Retrieval has no idea what’s out of date.

Grabs the nearest text

A plain-English query (“life insurance deduction”) lexically matches the friendly old booklet better than dense legalese — so it retrieves 80C and answers 80C.

No notion of authority

Booklet vs statute look identical to an embedding. Nothing tells the model one is repealed education and the other is governing law.

Exact tokens get blurred

Vector search smears rare anchors like “Schedule XV” or “Section 123”; pure dense retrieval misses the very thing that makes the answer correct.

Why “better” RAG still fails

Faithfulness ≠ correctness.

Most RAG quality scores (e.g. RAGAS faithfulness) only check that the answer is grounded in the retrieved text. A system that retrieves the stale booklet and answers “80C” scores 0.9+ on faithfulness — perfectly grounded, in the wrong source. The headline number flatters a confidently-wrong system.

That’s why we score a separate correctness gate: did the answer cite a repealed Act/section as current? Faithfulness can’t catch that; the gate can.
Our solution — high level

Currency-aware retrieval: prefer authoritative & current, on points of law.

Provenance on every chunk

Each chunk is tagged: statute → authoritative · in-force 2026; booklet → explanatory · 2020.

Authority-weighted selection

When a stale explainer and the statute both match, the statute leads — but only on points of law. Booklet-only questions (compounding, SIPs) still answer from the booklet.

Hybrid dense + keyword (RRF)

BM25 nails exact anchors (“Schedule XV”, section numbers) that embeddings blur; fused with dense recall.

Currency-aware generation

The model is told the statute governs and must never present a repealed section as current — and to decline cleanly when the answer isn’t in the documents.

Our solution — low level

The pipeline, end to end.

Query-expansion (old→new terms) → HyDE → Weaviate hybrid (BM25 + Jina vectors + RRF) → currency-aware selection → cross-reference resolution → grounded generation with refusal.

Try it

Ask LexRAG.

It answers only from two documents: the Income-tax Act, 2025 and the SEBI investor-education booklet. Ask about Indian income-tax deductions, residence, definitions, or basic investing concepts. Off-topic questions are declined.

Which section gives the deduction for life-insurance premiums and PPF? Does current law use “previous year” and “assessment year”? How many days make me a resident for tax? What is a Systematic Investment Plan? What’s the US federal standard deduction?
About this demo: the benchmark numbers above were measured with GPT-4o-mini + a CrossEncoder reranker. This live demo runs free open-source infrastructure — Groq (Llama 3.3) for generation, Jina embeddings + Weaviate hybrid search — so quality is a notch below the benchmarked stack, but the currency-aware behaviour is the same.
